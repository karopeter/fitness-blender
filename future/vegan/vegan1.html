One-Pot Whole Wheat Veggies Pasta - Easy
Healthy Dinner Recipe

W hen spring starts cropping up at the market, it seems to come all at once - gaint loads of
greens, bunches of fresh mint, endless rows of aspargus and boxes full of fresh green peas. One of
the best ways to make use of this bountiful sprint produce is through a simple pasta.

Related: We just released our new 4 Week Meal Plan; If you need some guidance on making the switch to
clean eating, this plan provides 3 meals & 3 snacks a day, using all eaasy, whole food recipes.

With your pasta, make sure to showcase the beautiful colors and brigh taste of fresh vegetables by doing as
little to them as possible (which also help retain more nutrients). Here, asparaqus, kale and peas (fresh or
frozen will work fine), are simply blanched so they become tender, but do not overcook, which will cause them
to lose their flavor.

By mixing your nutrient-dense greens with whole wheat pasta, which incorpotates the brain, germ and
endosperm of the wheat grain and thus packs in more fiber and health benefits, this recipe is much better for
you than your average pasta dish.

Some people aren't crazy about the taste or texture of whole grain pasta, but these days there are many
different kinds readily available in grocery stores, so play around with various brands until you find one you like.
You can even try the whole grain pasta other thab wheat, such as spelt quinoa, kamut or amaranth.

It's well worth opting for a whole grin pasta since increasing research shows how whole grains can heelp lower
the risk of heart disease, diabeted, cancer and obesity. Plus, this is another way to get in your recommended
daily whole grain intake (USDA recommends 3 servings per day) beyong the usual cereals and breads. Over
time, many people end up preferring whole grain pasta, since it brings a more complex flavor and a slight
nuttiness to the table. Also, since whole grain pasta has a low GI, it is suitable for diabetics.

For an added savory kick, the pasta is finished with some quality Parmesan. You don't need a lot of this - just a
sprinkling over the top. Of course, if you want to make it dairy free, you can ship the cheese. Bare in mind,
however, if you are on a lactose-free diet, true Parmigiano Reggiano is lactos free (thanks to the aging
process) - just be sure to check the packaging, which will say "lactos-free" if it is the real stuff.

Seve the paste warm, cold or at room temperature. Leftovers will keep will for the next day's lunch or dinner,
just be sure to seal it in an airtight container in the fridge. Give the pasta a squeeze of lemon jucie or drizzle of
oil before serving if it's been chilled and seems a bit dry.

1 package whole wheat spaghetti
1 bunch asparagus, trimmed and cut into 2-inch pieces
6 kale leaves, ribbed and roughly chopped (about 2 cups)
1 cup frozen or fresh peas
1/4 cup olive oil
1 clove garlic, minced
Grated Parmesan,for serving
Fresh mint leaves, to garnish
Salt, to tast
Pepper, to taste

Fill a large stockpot with water; bring to a boil. Drop in spaghetti and cook 5 mintues. Add asparagus, kale and
peas and continue to cook another 5 minutes or until pasta is al dente. Reserve 1 cup of pasta water and set
aside.

In a colander in the sink, strain spaghetti and vegetables.

Return pot to stove over medium heat. Add oil and garlic; saute until golden, about 2 minutes.

Return strained spagehetti and vegetables are light coated and shimmery. Trasnfer to a serving
platter or bowl and sprinkle with Parmesan and mint leaves. Season to taste with salt and pepper. Serve
immediately or allow to cool, store in a na airtight container and refrigerate up to 5 days.
